## 🧠 What Is This About? (1-2 sentences)

Day 2 covers the solution side of the behavioural design process: how to generate intervention ideas using frameworks like [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/EAST Framework - BIT\|EAST]] and [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/COM-B Model - Michie et al\|COM-B]], how to prioritise and prototype them, and how to evaluate whether they actually work using both qualitative and quantitative methods (including RCTs, quasi-experiments, and propensity score matching).

---

## 📌 Key Takeaways

- **BI Solutioning Part 1 — Concepts & Frameworks:** Recap of the "Understand" phase, then introduces behavioural solutioning frameworks. COM-B is mapped to specific intervention types. EAST (and a creative twist called "anti-EAST") is used as a practical solutioning checklist. Other frameworks mentioned too — the point is that frameworks give you a structured starting point for generating ideas, not a rigid formula.
- **BI Solutioning Part 2 — Ideation & Prioritisation:** Structured ideation sessions with rules (build on each other's ideas, no judgement, quantity over quality). Ideas are then plotted on an **impact-feasibility matrix** (2×2 grid) to prioritise the 1-2 ideas worth prototyping — high impact AND high feasibility wins.
- **BI Solutioning Part 3 — Prototyping:** Making ideas tangible before committing resources. Prototypes have four qualities to consider: form, fidelity (level of detail/polish), lifecycle (throwaway vs evolving), and purpose. Methods include sketches & storyboards, mock-ups & models, and wireframes & user flows.
- **Qualitative Evaluation:** Can be done pre-launch (pilot/concept testing) or post-launch (process evaluation). Methods include focus group discussions, interviews, and field observation. Compared to a "test screening" for a new film — you get rich feedback on why something works or doesn't, not just whether it does.
- **Quantitative Evaluation (Impact Evaluation):** Covers the hierarchy of evidence — from RCTs (gold standard) down through quasi-experimental designs. Four quasi-experimental techniques covered, including propensity score matching (creating matched control/treatment groups from survey data). Practical example using MOM brand campaign with 823 survey respondents.

## 💡 Insights Worth Remembering

- The **anti-EAST** concept is clever: instead of just asking "how do we make the desired behaviour Easy, Attractive, Social, Timely?" also ask "how do we make the undesired behaviour harder, less attractive, less social, and poorly timed?" It's the same framework flipped as a friction-adding tool.
- The **IKEA effect** is mentioned as a reason to co-create solutions with users rather than just design for them — people value things more when they've had a hand in building them.
- The impact-feasibility matrix is a simple but effective prioritisation tool: plot every idea on a 2×2 grid, then focus only on the top-right quadrant (high impact + high feasibility). Don't waste time on great ideas you can't actually implement.
- For prototyping, the training emphasises that low-fidelity prototypes (rough sketches, paper mock-ups) are often better than polished ones at this stage — they invite more honest feedback because people don't feel bad criticising something that looks unfinished.
- The quantitative evaluation section is refreshingly honest about what you can and can't conclude from different methods — quasi-experiments are positioned as practical alternatives when RCTs aren't possible, not as inferior substitutes.
