## 🧠 What Is This About? (1-2 sentences)

This paper introduces the Behaviour Change Wheel (BCW), a comprehensive framework built on top of the [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/COM-B Model - Michie et al\|COM-B model]]. It provides nine intervention functions and seven policy categories that practitioners can use to systematically design behaviour change interventions instead of relying on guesswork.

---

## 📌 Key Takeaways

- The authors reviewed 19 existing behaviour change frameworks and found that **none** were comprehensive, coherent, AND linked to a model of behaviour — so they built one that is.
- The BCW has three layers (like rings on a target):
    - **Centre (hub):** The COM-B behaviour system — Capability, Opportunity, Motivation → Behaviour
    - **Middle ring:** 9 intervention functions — Education, Persuasion, Incentivisation, Coercion, Training, Restriction, Environmental Restructuring, Modelling, Enablement
    - **Outer ring:** 7 policy categories — Communication/Marketing, Guidelines, Fiscal measures, Regulation, Legislation, Environmental/Social Planning, Service Provision
- Each intervention function maps to specific COM-B components. For example: if the barrier is physical capability, the relevant interventions are Training and Enablement. If the barrier is reflective motivation, Education, Persuasion, and Incentivisation are relevant.
- Policies enable interventions — for example, "Coercion" (an intervention function) could be delivered through "Fiscal measures" or "Legislation" (policy categories).

---

## 💡 Insights Worth Remembering

- The paper makes an important distinction: **"Education" is about knowledge, "Training" is about skills, and "Modelling" is about imitation.** In everyday language these blur together, but keeping them separate helps you design sharper interventions.
- **"Enablement"** is the catch-all for interventions that increase capability or opportunity through means beyond education, training, or environmental restructuring — things like medication, surgery, prosthetics, or comprehensive behavioural support programmes.
- The BCW approach asks: "What conditions need to be in place for the target behaviour to happen?" This is different from asking "What correlates with behaviour right now?" — it's a causal question, not just a statistical one.
- The BCW was tested for reliability using the UK's 2010 Tobacco Control Strategy and NICE Obesity Guidelines, achieving 79-88% inter-rater agreement — meaning different people applying the framework reach similar conclusions.
- The authors deliberately criticised the UK government's "Nudge" approach for only using a narrow slice of the BCW (environmental restructuring and subtle persuasion), while ignoring coercion, restriction, and other potentially effective functions.
