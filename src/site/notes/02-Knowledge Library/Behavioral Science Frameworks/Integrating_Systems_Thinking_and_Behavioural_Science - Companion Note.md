# Integrating Systems Thinking and Behavioural Science

## 🧠 What Is This About? (1-2 sentences)

This review argues that behavioural science (focused on individual decision-making) and systems thinking (focused on complex, interconnected wholes) should be integrated to tackle "wicked problems" like public health, sustainability, and social equity. It proposes design thinking as a practical bridge between the two disciplines and offers a structured Discover–Define–Develop–Deliver framework for doing so.

---

## 📌 Key Takeaways

- **Education-only interventions often fail** because they target slow, deliberative (Type 2) thinking while ignoring the fast, automatic (Type 1) processes that drive most daily behaviour — the "intention-action gap."
- **Individual behaviour change doesn't scale linearly.** Applying interventions designed for individuals to larger populations can produce unintended consequences because complex systems absorb or redirect those efforts in unpredictable ways.
- **Leverage points matter more than easy targets.** Most interventions focus on weak leverage points that feel tractable but have little real impact; effective change requires identifying where tension, disequilibrium, or momentum already exists in the system (Abson et al., 2017; Meadows, 1999).
- **Design thinking (Double Diamond) is a practical integrating framework.** Its Discover → Define → Develop → Deliver phases map neatly onto the combined toolkits of both disciplines — from system mapping and COM-B analysis through to co-designed, iterative interventions.
- **The i-frame vs. s-frame distinction is critical.** Chater & Loewenstein (2023) argue that placing the burden of change on individuals (i-frame) distracts from the systemic rules and structures (s-frame) that actually shape behaviour — e.g., you can't expect healthy eating when the food system incentivises the opposite.

---

## 💡 Insights Worth Remembering

- **Systems self-organise around equilibrium.** Classical interventions often have little impact because the system simply "absorbs" the change and returns to its steady state. The neuron-firing analogy is memorable: a system only shifts once accumulated tension crosses a threshold — a tipping point.
- **Narratives are an underrated analytical tool.** Snowden's approach of listening to stories within a system to detect where tension and disequilibrium exist offers a qualitative complement to traditional behavioural data collection. The idea of generating "more stories like this" as a change strategy is a powerful reframe.
- **~30% adoption may be enough for norm change.** Lab evidence suggests a new social norm can become pervasive once roughly 30% of a population adopts it — though this depends heavily on whether the behaviour happens to target an existing point of tension.
- **The Cynefin framework helps choose your approach.** Traditional behavioural science works best in the "Clear" domain (simple cause-and-effect); systems thinking is needed in the "Complicated" and "Complex" domains; in "Chaotic" situations (e.g., early COVID-19), neither applies and crisis response takes over.
- **RCTs may be too "brittle" for complex systems.** Schmidt & Stenger (2024) argue traditional RCTs may not transfer across contexts, may not find equilibrium in the larger system, and may only show short-term effects. Alternatives like SMART (Sequential Multiple Assignment Randomised Trial) and narrative-based evaluation are emerging.
