## 🧠 What Is This About? (1-2 sentences)

EAST is a practical framework from BIT that says if you want to encourage a behaviour, make it **Easy, Attractive, Social, and Timely**. It's a simple checklist for designing behavioural interventions, backed by a decade of real-world case studies and trials from around the world.

---

## 📌 Key Takeaways

- **Make it Easy:** Use defaults (people stick with the pre-set option), reduce friction/effort, and simplify messages. Even tiny barriers can stop people from acting.
- **Make it Attractive:** Draw attention through novelty and personalisation, frame the behaviour in appealing ways, and design incentives smartly (deposit contracts, gamification, prosocial incentives).
- **Make it Social:** Use social norms (show most people do the right thing), leverage networks to spread behaviour, and create feelings of reciprocity.
- **Make it Timely:** Intervene at the right moment (life transitions, fresh starts), consider that people weight immediate costs/benefits more than future ones, and help people make specific plans.
- Context matters hugely — what works in one setting may not work in another. Always test and evaluate.

---

## 💡 Insights Worth Remembering

- Defaults are wildly powerful: switching UK pensions from opt-in to opt-out added 10 million savers in 8 years, mostly benefiting lower-income workers.
- Novel information grabs attention better than familiar messages: telling patients a missed NHS appointment costs £160 reduced no-shows by 25%, while a vague "wasting NHS money" message didn't work as well.
- Social norms can backfire: if you accidentally signal that "most people are doing the wrong thing," you can make the problem worse. Robert Cialdini calls this "the big mistake."
- The "signing at the top increases honesty" finding from the original 2014 EAST report was retracted after evidence of data manipulation — a good reminder that replication matters.
- Behaviours spread through networks via "complex contagion" — people often need multiple contacts from different people before they adopt a new behaviour, not just one exposure.
- Financial incentives can crowd out intrinsic motivation (the Swiss nuclear waste facility example: offering money to accept it reduced acceptance from 51% to 25%).
