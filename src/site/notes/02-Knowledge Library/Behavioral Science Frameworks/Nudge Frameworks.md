# Nudge Frameworks

## 🧠 What Is This About? (1-2 sentences)

A "nudge" is any feature of the choice environment that predictably alters behaviour without forbidding options or significantly changing economic incentives (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008). Nudge frameworks are practitioner toolkits — like [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/EAST Framework - BIT\|EAST]] and MINDSPACE — that translate that idea into concrete intervention design checklists.

---

## 🧰 Key Frameworks

- **EAST (BIT, 2014/2024)** — Easy, Attractive, Social, Timely. See [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/EAST Framework - BIT\|EAST Framework - BIT]]. The most practitioner-friendly of the lot.
- **MINDSPACE (Institute for Government / BIT, 2010)** — Messenger, Incentives, Norms, Defaults, Salience, Priming, Affect, Commitments, Ego. Older but more comprehensive; useful when EAST feels too coarse.
- **NUDGES (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008)** — iNcentives, Understand mappings, Defaults, Give feedback, Expect error, Structure complex choices. The original taxonomy.
- **4Ps (OECD, 2017)** — Process, Person, Product, Persuasion. Used in regulatory contexts.

---

## 📌 Key Principles

- **Choice architecture** — every choice has a context (ordering, defaults, framing, salience). Designing that context deliberately is what nudging *is*. No "neutral" choice environment exists.
- **Defaults are the single most powerful lever** — opt-out consistently outperforms opt-in. See the UK pensions case in [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/EAST Framework - BIT\|EAST Framework - BIT]] (+10M savers in 8 years).
- **Friction works in both directions** — reduce it to encourage a behaviour ([[Friction Costs and Sludge\|Friction Costs and Sludge]]); add it to discourage one ("sludge audit", or the "anti-EAST" idea from [[02-Knowledge Library/Training Materials/MOM Introductory BI Programme - Day 2 (Design and Test)\|MOM Introductory BI Programme - Day 2 (Design and Test)]]).
- **Social proof is double-edged** — descriptive norms can backfire if they signal the *undesired* behaviour is common (Cialdini's "big mistake"). See [[Social Norms\|Social Norms]].
- **Nudges preserve liberty by definition** — restriction and coercion are separate intervention functions on the [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/Behaviour Change Wheel - Michie et al\|Behaviour Change Wheel]], not nudges.

---

## ⚠️ When Nudges Work — and When They Don't

**Work well when:**
- The behaviour involves a single, well-defined decision moment
- The barrier is friction, salience, or default-stickiness (low-motivation contexts)
- You can test and iterate

**Limitations:**
- **Narrow slice of the toolkit** — Michie et al. explicitly criticised "Nudge" for using only Environmental Restructuring and subtle Persuasion, ignoring the other 7 intervention functions on the [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/Behaviour Change Wheel - Michie et al\|BCW]].
- **Doesn't address structural causes** — the i-frame vs s-frame critique (Chater & Loewenstein, 2023): nudges put the burden on individuals rather than the systems shaping their choices. See [[02-Knowledge Library/Behavioral Science Frameworks/Integrating_Systems_Thinking_and_Behavioural_Science - Companion Note\|Integrating_Systems_Thinking_and_Behavioural_Science - Companion Note]].
- **Replication issues** — several high-profile findings (signing-at-the-top for honesty) have failed replication or been retracted.
- **Short-term, brittle effects** — Schmidt & Stenger (2024) argue nudge RCTs often don't generalise across contexts or find system equilibrium.
